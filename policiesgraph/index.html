A look at environmental policies in California

California has established ambitious greenhouse reduction goals, and regulators have crafted a broad array of regulations to meet them. Here is a snapshot of some of the most important goals and programs for combatting climate change.

Vehicle

Clean Cars Bill (Pavley)

Assembly Bill 1493

Approved: 2002
A law that required the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions from cars. Similar national regulations took effect in 2010.
AB 1493 Bill (PDF)
Vehicle

Low Carbon Fuel Standard (LCFS)

Executive Order S-1-07

Approved: 2007
A program to reduce the carbon intensity of gasoline and other fuels in the state (to 10% by 2020). The program began requiring carbon reductions in 2011.
Executive Order (PDF)
Vehicle

Zero Emission Vehicle program (ZEV)

Air Resources Board Program

Enacted: 1990
A program aimed at increasing the number of vehicles that use little or no gasoline on the road. First enacted in 1990, with increasing targets.
More info on program website
Electricity

Renewable Portfolio Standard

Senate Bill 1078

Created: 2002
An energy requirement for California’s major electric utilities to get 33 percent of their energy from renewable sources by 2020. (All three are above 20 percent currently.) SB 350, currently under consideration in the Legislature, would raise that requirement for 50 percent by 2030.
SB 1078 Bill (PDF)
Industry

Cap-and-trade

Assembly Bill 32 program

Began: 2012
a program born out of AB 32 that gives large polluters a target for greenhouse gas emissions, and allows them to buy permission to pollute through an auction process, or by trading with other companies. This year transportation fuels, such as gasoline, were added to the program, which now covers 85 percent of California’s economy.
More info on AB32
Buildings

Energy Efficiency

Title 24

Rules Drafted: 2008, 2013
California is continually updating building standards; the latest ones, drafted in 2013, require buildings to use 25 percent less energy than prior rules, drafted in 2008. California also aims to have all new buildings consume no energy, on net, by 2020, meaning that the buildings would also produce energy through means like solar panels, even as they consume it.
Title 24 program
Communities

Sustainable Communities

Senate Bill 375

Passed: 2008
California requires major metropolitan areas to plan for better public transit and reduced use of cars. This sprang out of a 2008 bill, SB 375.
SB 375 (PDF)
Law

Jerry Brown’s Executive Order

Executive Order B-30-15

Executed: April 2015
Gov. Brown has ordered that the state reduce greenhouse gas emissions 40 percent below 1990 levels by 2030. By extending the current targets beyond 2020, this clarifies the path to getting to 80 percent reductions by 2050.
