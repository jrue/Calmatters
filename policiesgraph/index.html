A snapshot of climate policies in California

California has established ambitious greenhouse gas reduction goals, and regulators have crafted a broad array of rules to meet them. Here are some of the most important programs and goals for combating climate change.

Vehicle

Low Carbon Fuel Standard (LCFS)

Executive Order S-1-07

Approved: 2007
A program to reduce the carbon intensity of gasoline and other fuels in the state (by 10% by 2020). The program began requiring carbon reductions in 2011.
Executive Order (PDF)
Electricity

Renewable Portfolio Standard

Senate Bill 1078

Created: 2002
A requirement that California’s electricity providers get 33 percent of their energy from renewable sources by 2020. (All three are above 20 percent currently.) SB 350, under consideration in the Legislature, would raise that requirement to 50 percent by 2030.
SB 1078 Bill (PDF)
Industry

Cap-and-trade

Assembly Bill 32 program

Began: 2013
A program born out of AB 32 that gives large polluters a target for greenhouse gas emissions reductions, and allows them to buy permission to pollute through an auction process, or by trading with other companies. In 2015, transportation fuels, such as gasoline, were added to the program, which now covers 85 percent of California’s emissions.

Buildings

Energy Efficiency

Title 24

Rules Drafted: 2008, 2014
California is continually tightening its building standards; the latest ones require residential buildings to use 25 percent less energy than prior rules. SB 350, currently under consideration in the Legislature, seeks to double the energy efficiency in buildings.
Title 24 program
Rule

Executive Order

Executive Order B-30-15

Signed: April 2015
Gov. Jerry Brown has ordered that the state reduce greenhouse gas emissions 40 percent below 1990 levels by 2030. By extending the current targets beyond 2020, this clarifies the path to getting to 80 percent reductions by 2050.

Law

Assembly Bill 32

AB 32

Passed: 2006
A landmark law that requires California to reduce its greenhouse gas emissions to 1990 levels by 2020. The law, known as California Global Warming Solutions Act of 2006, laid the foundation for the cap-and-trade program as well as the low-carbon fuel standard and a host of other programs.

Bill

Senate Bill 350

SB 350

Introduced: 2015 (pending)
A bill that aims to cut California’s energy use by 2030. Specifically, the bill calls for a reduction of petroleum use by 50 percent; a doubling of the energy efficiency in buildings; and a requirement for most California utilities to get 50 percent of their energy from renewable sources by 2030. The Senate passed the bill in June. The Assembly is now evaluating it.

Bill

Senate Bill 32

SB 32

Introduced: 2015 (pending)
This bill aims to cut greenhouse gas emissions in California by 80 percent by 2050. That target was already established in 2005, by an executive order signed by then-Governor Arnold Schwarzenegger, and SB 32 would codify it into law.
